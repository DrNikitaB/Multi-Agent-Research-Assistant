Generative AI is a subset of artificial intelligence designed to create new, original content—such as text, images, audio, video, code, or 3D designs—in response to prompts provided by a user [1, 4].

### How Generative AI Works
Generative AI functions by learning patterns from massive datasets of existing information [1, 4]. 

*   **Training:** These models are trained using deep learning and neural networks [1, 4]. During this process, the model learns millions or billions of parameters that allow it to predict or generate content that resembles the real-world examples it was trained on [1].
*   **Inference:** Once trained, the model uses these learned patterns to generate original outputs based on the specific prompts provided by a user [1].
*   **Foundation Models:** Many systems are built on "foundation models" created from large, unlabeled datasets, which serve as a base for performing various tasks [4]. These can be further adapted through "fine-tuning" on domain-specific data to meet specialized needs [1].

### Key Differences from Traditional AI
While traditional AI is often focused on making predictions or classifying data (such as determining if an email is spam or if a medical scan shows a tumor), generative AI is focused on creation [1, 3]. 

*   **Traditional AI:** Typically designed to perform a narrow range of tasks repetitively based on data analysis [4].
*   **Generative AI:** Capable of producing new, original content that is often indistinguishable from human-created work [1, 4].

### Modern Enhancements
To improve the quality and reliability of the content produced, modern generative AI systems often incorporate additional techniques:
*   **Agents:** These are semi-autonomous components that can reason, plan, and execute chains of tasks, such as querying databases or controlling external APIs [1].
*   **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG):** This technique allows the model to retrieve relevant, up-to-date documents at the time of a query. This helps "ground" the output in factual information and reduces the likelihood of the model producing inaccurate information (hallucinations) [1].
