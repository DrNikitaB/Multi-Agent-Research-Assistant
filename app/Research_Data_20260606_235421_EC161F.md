This research paper by Mariusz Kruk explores how 20 advanced English language students at a Polish university use mobile devices (smartphones and tablets) to support their language learning.

**Key findings include:**
*   **Usage Patterns:** Students primarily use mobile devices for convenience, speed, and accessibility. Smartphones are the most commonly used devices.
*   **Learning Focus:** All participants use their devices to practice English vocabulary. A smaller number of students use them for pronunciation, listening, reading, and grammar.
*   **Tools:** Students frequently utilize online dictionaries (e.g., diki, ColorDict), mobile apps (e.g., Duolingo, Fiszkoteka), and online resources like YouTube and TED.
*   **Impact:** 75% of participants reported that mobile devices helped them study more effectively and efficiently, often by allowing them to dedicate more time to learning or by facilitating quicker access to information.
*   **Teacher Involvement:** The study noted a lack of explicit guidance from teachers regarding the use of mobile devices in the classroom. Teachers did not typically recommend specific apps or design tasks requiring mobile device usage.
*   **Limitations:** The study highlights that many students engage in only intuitive or ad hoc use of their devices, and nearly half of the participants considered themselves inexperienced in using mobile technology for language study.

**Conclusion:**
The author concludes that while students are using mobile devices autonomously, their engagement could be significantly improved if teachers actively integrated mobile technology into their instruction, provided training, and designed specific tasks that leverage the affordances of these devices.
